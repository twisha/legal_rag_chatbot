Background Ability To Repay Determinations The Truth in Lending Act&thinsp; [ 1 ] and its implementing Regulation Z&thinsp; [ 2 ] require creditors to assess consumers' ability to repay before offering mortgages and certain open-end credit products. This statement emphasizes to creditors that these requirements may obligate consideration of a consumer's immigration status, especially where removal from the United States may disrupt the consumer's income. Under TILA and Regulation Z, before lending to consumers for dwelling-secured transactions like mortgages, creditors must make &ldquo;a reasonable and good faith determination at or before consummation that the consumer will have a reasonable ability to repay the loan according to its terms.&rdquo;&thinsp; [ 3 ] Regulation Z sets forth parameters that lenders must follow to make such &ldquo;reasonable and good faith&rdquo; determinations of a consumer's ability to repay. For instance, a creditor must consider &ldquo;the consumer's current or reasonably expected income or assets.&rdquo;&thinsp; [ 4 ] Those creditors relying on a consumer's current income must in turn consider their &ldquo;current employment status.&rdquo;&thinsp; [ 5 ] As the regulations make clear, employment can take a number of forms&mdash;including part time, seasonal, irregular, and the like. A creditor may rely on any such employment income to determine loan repayment ability, &ldquo;so long as the creditor considers those characteristics of the employment.&rdquo;&thinsp; [ 6 ] And for those creditors relying on the consumer's expected income in addition to or instead of current income, &ldquo;the expectation that income will be available for repayment must be reasonable and verified with third-party records that provide reasonably reliable evidence of the consumer's expected income.&rdquo;&thinsp; [ 7 ] Similarly, credit card issuers must &ldquo;consider[&thinsp;] the consumer's ability to make the required minimum periodic payments.&rdquo;&thinsp; [ 8 ] That consideration may be based on information &ldquo;provided by the consumer in connection with the account&rdquo; or &ldquo;obtained through third parties (subject to any applicable information-sharing rules).&rdquo;&thinsp; [ 9 ] And the information could include &ldquo;any current or reasonably expected income or assets of the consumer.&rdquo;&thinsp; [ 10 ] To ensure card issuers evaluate a consumer's ability-to-pay, regulations require that they &ldquo;establish and maintain reasonable written policies and procedures to consider the consumer's ability to make the required minimum payments under the terms of the account,&rdquo; which &ldquo;include treating any income . . . to which the consumer has a reasonable ( printed page 34608) expectation of access as the consumer's income.&rdquo;&thinsp; [ 11 ] Importantly, Regulation Z makes clear that, in evaluating a consumer's ability to repay based on expected employment income, creditors need only consider repayment ability based on what is known when the decision to extend credit is made or the credit is issued, depending on the credit type. [ 12 ] Commentary to mortgage-related regulations explains that &ldquo;[a] change in the consumer's circumstances after consummation . . . that cannot be reasonably anticipated from the consumer's application or the records used to determine a repayment ability is not relevant to determining a creditor's compliance with the rule.&rdquo;&thinsp; [ 13 ] Likewise, while card issuers may use statistically sound and empirically derived models to calculate future income to which the consumer has a reasonable expectation, [ 14 ] they need not make any particular predictions about the continued likelihood that a consumer will earn income in the absence of specific information that would reasonably allow them to make a determination about the nature of the income stream in the future. [ 15 ] However, if the information the creditor considers when the decision to extend credit is made or the credit is issued indicates that there will be a change in repayment ability after consummation, a creditor must consider that information in order to have reasonably assessed a borrower's ability to repay.
